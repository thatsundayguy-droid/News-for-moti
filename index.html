CFA Prep  ·  Concept of the Day
Today's Concept
Cost of Capital: WACC & the Build-up Approach

WACC = (E/V) x Re + (D/V) x Rd x (1-t). Every weight uses market values, not book values. The after-tax cost of debt Rd(1-t) reflects the interest tax shield — only relevant for tax-paying entities.

Cost of equity via CAPM: Re = Rf + Beta x (Rm - Rf). The equity risk premium (ERP) is the market return above the risk-free rate. Beta measures systematic risk — it cannot be diversified away.

Exam traps: (1) Using book-value weights instead of market-value weights — always wrong. (2) Forgetting to unlever/relever beta when capital structure differs between comparable and subject company. (3) Applying WACC to equity cash flows — WACC discounts FCFF; cost of equity discounts FCFE or dividends.

Why It Matters for L1 and Your BII Work
  • WACC is the core discount rate in DCF valuations — directly tested in both Equity and Corporate Finance at L1
  • In IPEV / infrastructure DCF contexts, the discount rate is typically WACC applied to FCFF — cost of equity alone would overstate the rate for levered assets
  • Beta relevering formula: Levered Beta = Unlevered Beta x [1 + (1-t) x (D/E)] — tested in item sets involving comparable company analysis
  • Higher D/E increases levered beta and therefore equity risk — examiner tests whether candidates adjust for this correctly in peer-group analysis
Practice Prompt
  • Rf = 6%, ERP = 5%, unlevered beta = 0.8, t = 25%, D/E = 0.5 — calculate WACC if Rd = 8% and E/V = 67%
  • If a firm issues more debt (D/E rises from 0.5 to 1.0), does WACC necessarily rise or fall? Explain the two competing effects.
