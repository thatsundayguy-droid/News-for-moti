CFA Prep  ·  Concept of the Day
Today's Concept
Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) & FCFF

FCFF (Free Cash Flow to the Firm) = NI + NCC + Int(1–t) – FCInv – WCInv. It represents cash available to all capital providers — both debt and equity — before financing costs.

FCFE (Free Cash Flow to Equity) = NI + NCC – FCInv – WCInv + Net Borrowing. This is cash available to equity holders after debt obligations are met.

Key relationship: FCFE = FCFF – Int(1–t) + Net Borrowing. A firm with no debt has FCFE = FCFF.

Exam trap: NCC (non-cash charges) are added back in both — depreciation is the most common. Increases in working capital are a use of cash and reduce FCF; examiners often reverse this sign to catch candidates.

Why It Matters for L1
  • FCFF is used with WACC; FCFE is used with cost of equity — choosing the wrong discount rate is a classic item-set error
  • For HFCs and NBFCs (relevant to your sector): working capital definition shifts — focus on operating loans receivable changes, not inventory
  • FCFF is capital-structure neutral; useful for comparing levered vs unlevered firms — tested in equity valuation vignettes
  • When Net Borrowing is positive (more debt raised than repaid), FCFE exceeds FCFF after-tax interest — check direction carefully
Practice Prompt
  • NI ₹500, Dep ₹80, CapEx ₹120, ΔWCX ₹30, Int ₹40, t=25%, Net Borrowing ₹50 — calculate FCFF and FCFE
  • If a firm's debt increases by ₹200 with no other change, does FCFF change? Does FCFE?
