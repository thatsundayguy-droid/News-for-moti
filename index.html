Today's Concept
Equity Valuation: Price Multiples & the P/E Ratio

P/E Ratio = Price per Share / EPS. The justified P/E (from the Gordon Growth Model) = (D1/E1) / (r − g) = Payout Ratio / (r − g). A higher payout ratio, lower required return, or higher sustainable growth each justify a higher P/E.

Trailing vs Leading P/E: Trailing uses last 12 months' EPS (historical); Leading uses next 12 months' EPS (forward). Leading P/E is preferred for valuation — it captures expectations. In declining earnings environments, trailing P/E overstates cheapness.

Exam trap: A high P/E doesn't always mean overvalued — it may reflect high expected growth (g) or low risk (r). Conversely, a low P/E may signal a value trap, not a bargain. Always decompose P/E using the justified P/E formula before concluding over/undervaluation.

Why It Matters for L1 and Your Equity Competition Work
  • P/E and P/B are the primary multiples tested in the Equity topic at L1 — justified multiples via DDM decomposition is a frequent item-set question
  • For Aptus Value Housing Finance: high ROE + low payout + high retention = high sustainable growth (g) → justifies premium P/B vs peers on Gordon Growth logic
  • P/B = ROE / (r − g) from Gordon Growth — HFCs with superior ROE structurally deserve higher P/B; examiner will test this direction
  • Sector comparison trap: comparing P/E of a high-growth AHFC (Aptus, Home First) to a low-growth large HFC (HDFC Bank mortgage book) without adjusting for g is analytically incorrect
Practice Prompt
  • Aptus: ROE 17%, retention 80%, r = 14% — calculate justified P/B and justified P/E (assume EPS Rs 40, payout ratio 20%)
  • If a peer HFC has identical P/E but lower ROE, which is more attractive and why? What does this imply for relative P/B?
