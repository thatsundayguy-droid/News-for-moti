Today's Concept
Fixed Income: Bond Pricing, Duration & Convexity

Bond Price = PV of coupons + PV of par. Price moves inversely with yield. When YTM rises, price falls — and vice versa. A bond trading above par is at a premium (coupon > YTM); below par is at a discount (coupon < YTM).

Duration measures price sensitivity to a 1% change in yield. Modified Duration = Macaulay Duration / (1 + YTM/m). Approximate price change = −Duration × ΔYTM × Price. Longer duration = greater sensitivity to interest rate moves.

Convexity is the curvature correction. For large yield changes, the linear duration approximation understates the price gain and overstates the price loss. Positive convexity is good — bonds with higher convexity outperform at both ends of a yield move.

Exam trap: Duration is always positive for standard bonds, but price change uses a negative sign (−Duration × ΔYTM). If yields rise by 100 bps and duration is 5, price falls approximately 5% — not rises. Candidates flip this sign regularly under exam pressure.

Why It Matters for L1 and Your BII Work
  • Fixed Income is one of the largest topic areas at CFA L1 — bond pricing, yield measures, and duration are guaranteed item-set territory
  • For infrastructure debt (BII's loan book): longer-tenor project finance loans (15-25 year) carry high duration — interest rate risk is a key sensitivity in DCF valuations
  • India's G-sec 10-year yield at ~6.88% — if RBI cuts further to 5%, long-duration bonds gain significantly; duration amplifies that gain
  • HFC NCD pricing is directly linked to G-sec yields plus a spread — duration of HFC bonds and liability matching is a core treasury management concept
Practice Prompt
  • Bond: 8% coupon, 10-year maturity, semi-annual, YTM 9%. Is it trading at premium or discount? Calculate approximate price change if YTM falls 50 bps and Modified Duration = 6.8 years
  • Two bonds have identical duration but different convexity. Which outperforms if yields move sharply in either direction? Why?
